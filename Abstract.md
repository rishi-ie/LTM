# Abstract

Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities by scaling autoregressive next-token prediction over massive corpora. Despite their success, their fundamental computational paradigm remains sequential: reasoning is expressed as the generation of language rather than as an explicit optimization over knowledge itself. Consequently, knowledge representation, reasoning, memory, and language generation remain tightly coupled within a single parameterized system, causing inference complexity to grow with reasoning length while making continual knowledge integration and explicit reasoning control difficult.

This thesis proposes the **Latent Topology Model (LTM)**, a fundamentally different computational architecture in which reasoning is formulated as **continuous geometric optimization over a learned cognitive topology** rather than autoregressive token generation. The central hypothesis of LTM is that intelligence is not primarily a property of sequential symbolic computation, but rather a property of the geometry of a continuously learned latent space. Within this framework, language becomes an interface to cognition rather than the computational substrate of cognition itself.

Unlike conventional embedding models that organize latent representations according to semantic similarity, LTM introduces a **First-Principles Cognitive Topology** that organizes knowledge according to universal cognitive invariants. Rather than asking whether two concepts are semantically similar, the topology captures how cognitive states participate in reasoning through their constraints, causal relationships, consequence structures, dependencies, conflicts, abstractions, goals, and evidential support. These relationships define a unified geometric structure in which arbitrary domains—including mathematics, physics, biology, software engineering, economics, and natural language—coexist within a common latent manifold governed by identical cognitive principles.

To represent directed reasoning, LTM replaces traditional symmetric embeddings with a **Dual-Vector Asymmetric Representation**. Every cognitive state is encoded as an ordered pair consisting of an **Origin Vector**, representing the state as a causal premise or antecedent, and a **Target Vector**, representing the same state as a logical consequence or resolved conclusion. This asymmetric representation explicitly models the directional nature of implication, allowing forward consequence propagation to emerge naturally while avoiding the limitations of symmetric semantic distances.

Building upon this representation, LTM introduces the concept of a **Consequence Topology**, a continuous latent manifold in which every cognitive state is connected not merely by semantic association but by recursively propagating consequence relationships. States become geometrically proximal when they converge toward similar downstream consequences or satisfy similar cognitive constraints, rather than when they merely share linguistic context. Consequently, the topology represents a continuous organization of causal, logical, and constraint-driven interactions instead of a statistical organization of words.

The central computational mechanism of LTM is a **Global Reasoning Field**, a continuous vector field induced collectively by every embedded cognitive state. Rather than performing explicit retrieval, graph traversal, symbolic search, or autoregressive generation, inference proceeds by placing an encoded cognitive state into this field and allowing it to evolve according to locally evaluated cognitive forces. Each embedded state contributes to the global field through its structural relationships, while only the locally relevant components exert significant influence during optimization. This principle of **Global Influence, Local Computation** decouples inference from explicit knowledge traversal and hypothesizes that reasoning complexity depends primarily on optimization dynamics rather than total stored knowledge.

Within this framework, reasoning is formulated as the minimization of a **Composite Cognitive Energy Function**. Cognitive energy quantifies global inconsistency through violations of constraints, causal incoherence, unresolved dependencies, logical conflicts, incomplete consequence structures, abstraction inconsistencies, and goal distance. Low-energy regions correspond to geometrically stable reasoning attractors, while inconsistent or contradictory cognitive configurations form high-energy repulsive regions. Rather than generating intermediate reasoning chains, the system continuously evolves toward cognitive equilibrium by minimizing this energy landscape.

Inference is modeled as a continuous dynamical system governed by a **Neural Ordinary Differential Equation (Neural ODE)**. The encoded cognitive state evolves under the influence of the global reasoning field, optionally augmented by Langevin dynamics to escape shallow local minima within complex reasoning landscapes. Optimization proceeds until the gradient of the cognitive energy approaches zero, indicating convergence to a stable low-energy equilibrium. Only after convergence is a language decoder invoked to translate the final latent cognitive state into natural language. Consequently, language generation occurs exactly once and functions solely as the external expression of an already completed reasoning process.

A fundamental consequence of this formulation is the explicit separation of **knowledge representation**, **reasoning dynamics**, and **language generation** into independent computational modules. New information is incorporated by embedding it into the cognitive topology according to its structural consistency with existing cognitive relationships rather than through complete retraining of the reasoning mechanism. As additional knowledge is embedded, the topology reorganizes locally, refining the global reasoning field while preserving previously learned cognitive structure. This architecture therefore naturally supports continual learning, topology refinement, and recursive self-improvement without requiring wholesale reconstruction of the underlying reasoning engine.

From a computational perspective, LTM hypothesizes a fundamentally different scaling law from autoregressive architectures. Whereas transformer inference scales with generated sequence length and repeated sequential forward passes, LTM proposes that inference complexity depends primarily upon the number of latent optimization iterations required to reach cognitive equilibrium. Knowledge expansion increases the geometric richness of the cognitive topology rather than the computational complexity of individual reasoning episodes. If validated experimentally, this would establish a new computational regime in which increasingly capable reasoning systems can incorporate arbitrarily large knowledge bases without proportional increases in inference cost.

The central scientific contribution of this thesis is therefore not the introduction of a new neural architecture alone, but the proposal of an alternative computational foundation for intelligence. Specifically, this work advances the hypothesis that general reasoning can emerge from the interaction between (i) a learned first-principles cognitive topology, (ii) a globally induced reasoning field generated by embedded knowledge, and (iii) continuous latent optimization toward stable cognitive equilibria. Under this formulation, intelligence is reinterpreted as the evolution of cognitive states within a structured geometric field rather than the sequential prediction of linguistic symbols.

The validity of the Latent Topology Model ultimately reduces to a set of experimentally testable scientific questions: whether such a first-principles cognitive topology exists, whether arbitrary knowledge can be consistently embedded within it, whether a globally induced reasoning field can be learned from data, whether continuous latent optimization produces reasoning behaviors that generalize beyond autoregressive generation, and whether the resulting architecture exhibits favorable computational scaling with increasing knowledge. If these hypotheses are confirmed, LTM would represent a new class of foundation model in which cognition is fundamentally geometric, optimization-driven, and continuously self-organizing, providing a potential post-autoregressive framework for general artificial intelligence.
